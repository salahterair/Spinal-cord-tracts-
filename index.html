Part 1: Introduction to Spinal Cord Tracts

The spinal cord is the principal conduit between the brain and the rest of the body. Its white matter is organised into discrete bundles of nerve fibres called tracts, each dedicated to transmitting a specific category of information in one direction. Mastering this organisation is essential for understanding both normal neurological function and the clinical patterns produced by injury.

1.1 White Matter Organisation

The white matter of the spinal cord is divided into three pairs of funiculi (columns): the posterior (dorsal), lateral, and anterior (ventral) funiculi. Tracts within these funiculi are grouped into two broad functional categories:

  • Descending (motor) tracts: carry motor commands from higher brain centres down to the anterior horn cells (AHC) of the spinal cord, which then drive skeletal muscle.
  • Ascending (sensory) tracts: carry sensory information gathered from receptors in the skin, muscles, joints, and viscera upward to the brainstem, cerebellum, and cerebral cortex.
1.2 Classification Overview
CategoryMain TractsPrimary Function
Descending (Motor)Corticospinal (Pyramidal), Extrapyramidal group, CorticobulbarVoluntary movement, posture, reflex modulation, cranial nerve control
Ascending (Sensory)Dorsal columns, Spinothalamic, SpinocerebellarProprioception, touch, pain, temperature, coordination

Understanding whether a tract crosses the midline (decussates), and at what level, is the key to interpreting clinical findings after spinal cord injury.

Part 2: Descending Motor Tracts

Voluntary movement is not produced by a single pathway. Two complementary systems — the pyramidal and extrapyramidal tracts — work in close anatomical proximity and functional synergy to produce coordinated, purposeful movement.

2.1 The Pyramidal Tract (Corticospinal Tract)

The pyramidal tract is the principal descending motor pathway responsible for voluntary, especially fine skilled, movement. It takes a direct, largely uninterrupted route from the cerebral cortex to the anterior horn cells of the spinal cord.

Origin and Composition

  • 30% from the primary motor cortex (Area 4)
  • 30% from the premotor cortex (Area 6)
  • 40% from the somatic sensory cortices (Areas S I and S II)

Anatomical Pathway

  • Cortex → Corona Radiata → Internal Capsule → Midbrain → Pons → Medullary Pyramids
  • Motor Decussation: ~80% of fibres cross at medulla-spinal cord junction
2.2 The Corticobulbar Tract

The corticobulbar tract controls voluntary movements of the head, face, and neck via cranial nerve nuclei in the brainstem.

Key Features

  • Origin: Lateral motor cortex (precentral gyrus)
  • Termination: Cranial nerve motor nuclei (III, IV, V, VI, VII, IX, X, XI, XII)
  • Most fibres decussate at the level of their target nucleus
Important: Lower facial nucleus receives only contralateral innervation, causing forehead-sparing weakness in UMN lesions.
2.3 The Extrapyramidal Tracts

The extrapyramidal system encompasses all descending motor pathways other than the corticospinal tract, relaying through multiple subcortical structures.

Part 3: Motor Neuron Lesions — UMN vs. LMN

Motor neuron lesions are classified by anatomical level relative to the anterior horn cell (AHC). This distinction determines the clinical syndrome.

3.1 Upper Motor Neuron Lesion (UMNL)

Damage to descending motor fibres above the AHC level.

Characteristic Signs

  • Spasticity (clasp-knife phenomenon)
  • Exaggerated deep tendon reflexes
  • Babinski's sign present
  • Widespread paralysis (hemiplegia/monoplegia)
  • Minimal muscle atrophy
3.2 Lower Motor Neuron Lesion (LMNL)

Damage to the AHC, ventral nerve root, or peripheral motor nerve.

Characteristic Signs

  • Localised flaccid paralysis
  • Loss of all muscle tone (flaccidity)
  • Absent reflexes
  • Marked muscle atrophy
  • Fasciculations may be present
3.3 Spinal Shock

Occurs immediately after acute UMN lesion (e.g., spinal cord transection).

  • Transient flaccid paralysis below the lesion
  • Loss of all reflexes
  • Duration: typically 1-3 weeks
  • Gradually transitions to classic UMN syndrome
Clinical Note: Flaccidity in acute phase does NOT rule out UMN lesion.

Part 4: Ascending Sensory Tracts

Ascending tracts carry sensory information from the body to higher brain centres. Sensations may be conscious or unconscious.

4.1 The Three-Neuron Chain

Most ascending sensory pathways share a common three-neuron architecture:

OrderCell Body LocationKey Details
1st-order neuronPosterior (dorsal) root ganglionPeripheral receptor to cord entry
2nd-order neuronPosterior grey column (dorsal horn)Usually decussates and ascends to thalamus
3rd-order neuronThalamus (VPL nucleus)Projects to primary somatosensory cortex
4.2 Dorsal Column Pathway (Medial Lemniscal System)

Transmits discriminative sensations: fine touch, vibration, conscious proprioception.

Key Anatomical Features

  • Ipsilateral ascent in posterior white column without synapsing in cord
  • Fasciculus gracilis: lower limb and trunk (T7 and below)
  • Fasciculus cuneatus: upper limb and trunk (T6 and above)
  • Synapse in medulla: nucleus gracilis and nucleus cuneatus
  • Decussation: crosses as medial lemniscus to contralateral VPL thalamus
4.3 Spinothalamic Tracts

Primary conduits for pain, temperature, and crude touch. Crosses midline within spinal cord at entry level.

TractModalitiesPathway Notes
Lateral SpinothalamicPain and temperatureCrosses within 1–2 segments. Ascends in lateral funiculus.
Anterior SpinothalamicCrude touch and pressureCrosses near entry level. Ascends in anterior funiculus.
4.4 Spinocerebellar Tracts

Carry unconscious proprioceptive signals to cerebellum for coordination.

Posterior Spinocerebellar Tract

  • Origin: Clarke's column (C8–L3)
  • Course: Uncrossed ascent in posterolateral white column
  • Entry: Inferior cerebellar peduncle
  • Function: Lower limb and trunk proprioception

Anterior Spinocerebellar Tract

  • Origin: Clarke's column or dorsal horn
  • Course: Crosses in cord, then crosses back in cerebellum (double crossing)
  • Entry: Superior cerebellar peduncle
  • Result: Ipsilateral cerebellar termination

Part 5: Clinical Correlations

Different pathological conditions produce distinct patterns of deficits based on which tracts are damaged.

5.1 Brown-Séquard Syndrome (Cord Hemisection)

Damage to one lateral half of the spinal cord produces dissociated sensory loss.

Pattern: Ipsilateral loss of proprioception/vibration/touch + Ipsilateral UMN signs + Contralateral loss of pain/temperature
5.2 Central Cord Syndrome

Most common incomplete spinal cord injury; typically from hyperextension in cervical spondylosis.

  • Upper limbs > Lower limbs (motor weakness)
  • Dissociated sensory loss with sacral sparing
  • Pain/temperature loss in cape-like distribution
5.3 Anterior Cord Syndrome

Results from anterior spinal artery occlusion or anterior 2/3 cord damage.

  • Bilateral spastic paralysis (corticospinal damage)
  • Bilateral pain/temperature loss (spinothalamic damage)
  • Preserved vibration/proprioception (dorsal columns spared)
5.4 Posterior Cord Syndrome

Rare but clinically important syndrome affecting dorsal columns.

  • Loss of vibration, proprioception, fine touch
  • Preserved pain/temperature sensation
  • Sensory ataxia with positive Romberg's sign
5.5 Syringomyelia

Fluid-filled cavity within spinal cord preferentially destroys crossing spinothalamic fibres.

  • Bilateral loss of pain/temperature at lesion level
  • Preserved vibration/proprioception (dissociated sensory loss)
  • Cape-like distribution (upper limbs/shoulders first)
Diagnostic hallmark: Cannot feel pin/heat but can feel cotton swab.
5.6 Tabes Dorsalis

Late neurosyphilis causing bilateral degeneration of posterior roots and dorsal columns (particularly lumbosacral).

  • Loss of vibration and position sense
  • Loss of muscle stretch reflexes
  • Positive Romberg's sign
  • Ataxic (stamping) gait
  • Pain/temperature sensation INTACT
5.7 Subacute Combined Degeneration (SCD)

Progressive degeneration of dorsal and lateral columns from vitamin B12 deficiency.

  • Bilateral loss of vibration/proprioception (dorsal columns)
  • Spastic weakness with UMN signs (corticospinal tracts)
  • Sensory ataxia with positive Romberg's sign
  • Peripheral neuropathy may be present
5.8 Spinal Cord Compression

Common cause of myelopathy from tumours, abscesses, haematomas, or disc herniation.

  • Sensory level (distinct level below which sensation impaired)
  • UMN signs below compression level
  • LMN signs at compression level
  • Late bowel/bladder dysfunction

Common Causes

  • Metastatic carcinoma (most common)
  • Meningioma
  • Neurofibroma
  • Disc herniation
  • Spinal epidural abscess
5.9 Stroke and Hemiplegia Patterns

Stroke patterns reflect anatomy of decussation and fibre arrangement at each level.

  • Cortical/corona radiata lesion: Monoplegia (specific body part affected)
  • Internal capsule lesion: Dense contralateral hemiplegia
  • Brainstem lesion: Crossed (alternating) hemiplegia with ipsilateral cranial nerve involvement
